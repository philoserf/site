---
title: DNA as Remix Culture
description: Analyzes how framing genetic engineering through hip-hop sampling rhetoric shifts power, consent, and ownership questions onto the body.
date: 2026-07-27
---

> SPECULATIVE MACHINES, [Bios O.S.](https://soundcloud.com/speculative-machines/bios-os)  
> Our biochemical instructions are there for the taking. Like a choice James Brown sample, our DNA will be re-sampled, recycled, glitched, chopped and screwed.  

The claim that our biochemical instructions are there for the taking—that like a choice James Brown sample our DNA will be re-sampled, recycled, glitched, chopped and screwed—swaps one rhetorical register for another. Genetic engineering usually arrives wrapped in the language of Prometheus or the lab coat: hubris and white-room precision, designer babies and optimization curves. Here it arrives through the vocabulary of the MPC and the turntable, a creative tradition built on taking what was already made and finding what else it could do.

Brown earns the reference, and loads it. His grooves—"Funky Drummer," "Funky President," "Give It Up or Turnit a Loose"—are among the most-sampled sounds in recorded music, foundational to hip-hop, drum and bass, jungle and decades of pop. Early on, producers took them without permission and usually without payment; the samples built whole genres while Brown went uncredited and unpaid. Casting DNA as a choice Brown sample therefore carries both the generative promise of remix culture and its unease about consent, ownership and the flow of value. Genetic material as found object, treated as commons, with all the freedom and all the appropriation that implies.

The verbs carry the rest. Re-sampled and recycled are gentle words; they describe reuse, continuation, the way material persists through new contexts. Glitched embraces error and finds beauty where the system breaks. Chopped and screwed is specific: DJ Screw's Houston technique of slowing records to a syrupy crawl and cutting them across two decks, a vernacular innovation made on consumer gear. The list runs from preservation toward deliberate distortion. What begins as faithful reuse ends as something the original would barely recognize as kin.

The comparison is more accurate than its packaging lets on. Sexual reproduction has always been sampling—two libraries shuffled and recombined, with copy errors that occasionally turn out to be features. Evolution is glitch aesthetics on geological time. It feels transgressive only because the engineers are not introducing remix logic to pristine code; they are joining a remix that has run for four billion years, shortening the cycle and adding new producers to the credits.

What unsettles, then, is less the technical claim than the tonal one. The cheer is suspicious. It stays upbeat about something that might warrant more ambivalence: the dissolution of authorship at the most intimate level, the body as raw material in someone else's track. Whether one hears liberation or warning depends on whether one identifies with the sampler or the sampled—and Brown's own story, in which his beats made fortunes for others while he fought for recognition and royalties, suggests that question is rarely answered evenly.
