---
title: Human-LLM-PKM Collaboration
description: Argues that LLMs shift personal knowledge bases from archives to workshops by offloading reference storage to AI.
date: 2026-07-17
---

Human thought has always leaned on tools. The Greeks built memory palaces; Renaissance readers kept commonplace books; both were attempts to think beyond the skull's capacity. The newest tool changes the arrangement. Three kinds of intelligence can now work together: a human thinker, a personal knowledge base such as an Obsidian vault, and a large language model.

Each does something the others cannot. The human supplies judgment, taste, and the ability to tell a meaningful connection from a coincidental one. The vault holds the ideas a person has decided to keep, linked in ways that reflect how that person actually thinks. The LLM synthesizes and connects across more material than any individual could read.

The consequence for note-taking is direct. The old discipline of personal knowledge management assumed that anything worth knowing had to be captured, filed, and linked by hand, because nothing else would surface it later. That assumption no longer holds. The LLM is a universal commonplace book, able to supply context, connections, and source material on demand. Collecting everything becomes redundant work.

What remains worth keeping is smaller and more valuable: the ideas a person is developing, the projects in motion, the judgments and connections that are genuinely one's own. The vault stops being an archive and becomes a workshop. Its test is no longer completeness but use—does this note advance something being made?

The division of labor follows. Let the LLM hold the world's knowledge and retrieve it when asked. Let the vault hold the work in progress, shaped by one mind's purposes. Let the human do what neither machine can: decide what matters, see what others have missed, and turn material into finished work. Time once spent filing goes instead to thinking.

This is not a promise of wisdom on demand. The LLM retrieves and recombines; it does not judge. A workshop full of borrowed insight produces nothing without a craftsman. But the craftsman who no longer has to be a librarian gets more hours at the bench—and that, modestly, is the point.
