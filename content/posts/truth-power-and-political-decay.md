---
title: Truth, Power, and Political Decay
description: Traces erosion of shared factual ground in U.S. politics from Bush v. Gore through election denial, via Orwell and Bakunin.
date: 2026-08-10
---

Orwell's warnings about the manipulation of history and truth have grown newly relevant in American politics since the Bush v. Gore decision in 2000. That decision marked, for many, the moment institutions began treating outcomes as more negotiable than facts. The Iraq War rested on intelligence later shown to be wrong; the phrase "Homeland Security" recast a federal bureaucracy in the language of instinct rather than policy; two decades later, a president's supporters denied an election he had lost. Each episode wore away the shared factual ground that democratic argument requires.

Reporters and officials who challenged these episodes paid for it. Critics of the Iraq War were called unpatriotic. Consolidation among television networks narrowed which challenges reached a broad audience, even as cable news and social media multiplied the number of competing narratives fighting for attention. Inside government and party structures, dissent carried real professional cost, which made contesting the dominant story more expensive than repeating it. Bakunin's warning holds here: centralized power defends itself before it defends truth or freedom.

Bakunin did not believe such institutions could be reformed from within. His phrase, rendered roughly as "the passion for destruction is a creative passion," treated the collapse of a corrupt order as a kind of creation in itself, with no blueprint for what would follow. The idea offers little comfort. Dismantling a corrupt structure does not determine the shape of what replaces it, and the vacuum it leaves tends to reward whoever moves fastest, not whoever means best.

The institutions that mastered the distortion of reality face a final irony: the tools built to shape belief also erode the trust those institutions need to govern. It is the shape of the ouroboros, a mechanism that consumes the body that built it. A genuine strain of nihilism complicates the picture further, destruction pursued for its own satisfaction, indifferent to what comes after.
