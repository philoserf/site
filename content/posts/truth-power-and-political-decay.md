---
title: Truth, Power, and Political Decay
description: Traces erosion of shared factual ground in U.S. politics from Bush v. Gore through election denial, via Orwell and Bakunin.
date: 2026-08-10
---

Orwell's political warning was not merely that governments lie. Governments have always lied. The deeper danger was that power might damage the means by which a lie could be exposed, leaving citizens without enough common reality to argue about what should be done.

Bush v. Gore offers a useful, if imperfect, starting point for the American version of that decline. The Supreme Court's intervention ended the Florida recount and settled the presidency, but it did not settle the argument over the decision's legitimacy. For its critics, the case suggested that institutional authority could impose an outcome without persuading the public that the process had discovered the truth. The lesson was not that facts no longer mattered. It was that power could sometimes make unresolved facts politically irrelevant.

The invasion of Iraq made the problem harder to dismiss. The intelligence was not merely mistaken in hindsight. Later Senate investigations found that administration officials had repeatedly overstated the evidence, concealed uncertainty and presented unsupported claims as fact. Critics who questioned the case for war were often treated not as participants in democratic argument but as impediments to national unity. Patriotism became a test of agreement rather than loyalty to the country's interests.

Even the name "Homeland Security" helped change the terms of debate. It placed a new bureaucracy inside the image of a threatened household. A policy disagreement could then sound like indifference to the safety of home. The phrase did not abolish argument, but it gave one side possession of the emotional ground before the argument began.

By 2020 the same habit had escaped the institutions that had once used it. Donald Trump lost the election by 306 electoral votes to 232, yet he and many of his supporters denied the result. Official reviews found no evidence that foreign interference had altered the outcome, and the president's own officials repeatedly told him that his fraud claims were false. The dispute was no longer over the meaning of agreed facts. It was over whether an unwanted result could count as a fact at all.

Bakunin helps clarify the danger, though not in the way his most famous sentence is usually used. "The passion for destruction is a creative passion, too" appeared in an 1842 essay which treated the destruction of a reactionary order as the clearing of ground for another. Bakunin was not praising wreckage for its own sake. He believed the old order prevented the new from being born. His weakness lay elsewhere: destruction does not itself choose what follows. The fall of a corrupt structure rewards those ready to seize the opening, who may be neither free nor just.

Institutions that manipulate reality therefore face a danger more severe than exposure. They teach everyone else to regard truth as another instrument of power. Once that lesson has spread, corrections sound like propaganda, authority looks like fraud and every defeat invites invention. The system loses not only the public's trust but its ability to restore trust by telling the truth.

Nihilism begins where Bakunin's hope ends. It does not destroy a false order to release a better one. It destroys because destruction proves that something can still be done. A democracy can survive lies while enough people still expect truth to matter. It enters deeper trouble when disbelief itself becomes a source of pleasure and power.
