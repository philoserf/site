---
title: The Backup Club Effect
description: How golfers cycle between clubs via selective memory reveals a universal bias toward absent tools over present ones.
date: 2026-08-03
---

The backup club enjoys one profound advantage: it is not currently disappointing us.

A golfer buys Driver A. It performs well. Confidence grows and good shots follow. Then come a few poor rounds: a slice into the trees, a snap hook on a narrow hole, a string of drives that desert their owner at precisely the wrong moment. Faith erodes.

Fortunately, Driver B is waiting in the garage.

Driver B is more than a club. It is a possibility. It carries no recent disappointments and has accumulated no evidence against itself. The golfer puts it into play and finds a few encouraging results. The swing feels freer. Expectations are lower. Attention sharpens. A handful of good drives appear.

The verdict seems obvious: Driver B was the answer all along.

Then Driver B acquires a history. Missed fairways accumulate. Confidence fades. One day the golfer looks towards the garage again.

There sits Driver A.

Its bad shots have faded from memory. What remains is the towering drive on the 18th, the perfect draw around the dogleg, the round when everything clicked. Driver A has become noble in absence, remembered not as it was but as it might have been. The cycle begins again.

Putters make the pattern plainest. Many golfers own several and rotate them according to forces only loosely connected to physics. One is dependable until it is not. Another returns from years of exile and suddenly feels wonderful. Nothing has changed in the club. What has changed is the history the golfer brings to it.

The cycle depends on three small deceptions.

First, memory favours the absent club. The golfer using Driver B must live with every fresh mistake it produces. Driver A, meanwhile, exists only in recollection, where poor shots fade faster than great ones.

Second, novelty alters attention. A new or newly restored club interrupts habit. The golfer grips it more carefully, notices the face, commits to the target and makes a swing rather than merely repeating one. The improvement is real, but its cause may not be where the golfer thinks it is.

Third, expectation affects movement. A club burdened with recent failure produces caution, steering and tension. A club without that burden allows the hands to move. What feels like better technology may be renewed belief.

Equipment still matters. Drivers differ in length, loft, weight, forgiveness and fit. A club poorly suited to a golfer can make the game harder, and a better one can help. The mistake lies in treating every temporary improvement as proof that the new club caused it. Sometimes the tool is better. Often the change itself does most of the work.

The pattern is not confined to golf. An old job improves after we leave it. Its frustrations lose their edge, while the competent moments, friendships and small freedoms remain. The current job must bear the full weight of daily experience. The old one survives as an edited memory.

Distance removes friction. Absence forgives.

This is why changing tools can feel like changing oneself. The new system carries none of the old system's failures. The new place has not yet become ordinary. The new plan has not encountered resistance. Possibility remains clean because experience has not had time to soil it.

A golfer may find this discouraging. No permanent answer waits in the garage. No club can remove uncertainty from a game built upon it.

It may also be liberating. The search can stop. The player can choose sound equipment, learn its limits and stop asking it to carry responsibilities that belong to the swing.

Players of hickory golf encounter this lesson quickly. Their clubs are often decades older than they are. The equipment is fixed, limited and plainly imperfect. There is little room for fantasies about revolutionary technology. A hickory club will not rescue a poor decision or turn every awkward lie into a standard shot.

Attention therefore shifts from replacement to adaptation. The question is no longer, "How can this club save me?" It becomes, "What shot does this club invite me to play?"

That change is larger than it sounds. The golfer must notice the lie, the wind, the shape of the hole and the club's actual capacities. The equipment no longer promises a better future. It returns the player to the stroke in front of him.
