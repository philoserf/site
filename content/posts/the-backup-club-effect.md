---
title: The Backup Club Effect
description: How golfers cycle between clubs via selective memory reveals a universal bias toward absent tools over present ones.
date: 2026-08-03
---

Golfers have a peculiar relationship with their equipment. On the surface the game appears to be about clubs, balls, shafts, lofts, lie angles and swing weights, and enough time around a driving range or pro shop can convince anyone that success depends on finding the right tool. Yet a curious pattern emerges among experienced players.

A golfer buys Driver A. It performs well, confidence grows, good shots follow. Then a few poor rounds arrive: a slice into the trees, a snap hook on a narrow hole, a string of drives that abandon their owner at precisely the wrong moment. Faith erodes. Fortunately, Driver B is waiting in the garage. Driver B is not merely a piece of equipment but a possibility. It carries no recent disappointments, has accumulated no evidence against itself. It carries no failures yet.

The golfer puts Driver B into play and, almost inevitably, finds a few encouraging results. The swing feels freer, expectations are lower, attention sharpens, a handful of good drives appear. The verdict seems obvious: Driver B was the answer all along. Then time passes. Driver B acquires its own history of failures, the missed fairways accumulate, the confidence fades, and eventually the golfer looks toward the garage again.

There sits Driver A.

Something remarkable has happened. The bad shots have largely disappeared from memory, and the golfer remembers the towering drive on the 18th, the perfect draw around the corner of a dogleg, the round when everything clicked. Driver A has become noble in absence, recalled not as it was but as it might have been. The cycle begins anew.

Most golfers recognize this in themselves, even if they laugh about it. Putters may be the most extreme case: many players own several that rotate in and out of service according to forces only loosely connected to physics. One putter is dependable until it is not; another is resurrected from a closet after years of exile and suddenly feels wonderful again. The clubs had not changed. The golfer had.

The pattern extends far beyond golf. We blame the thing we are currently using for every frustration while granting the thing we are not using the benefit of selective memory. The previous version of the software becomes a masterpiece; the old job seems ideal while the current one stifles; the city we left behind grows more attractive with every passing year. Distance edits memory and absence removes friction. The difficulties of the present are vivid because we are carrying them; the difficulties of the past fade because we no longer have to. The backup club enjoys one profound advantage: it is not currently disappointing us.

We are storytelling creatures before we are analytical ones. We assemble narratives from incomplete information and seek causes for outcomes, especially when those outcomes matter. When things go poorly we search for explanations, and the club, the tool, the process, the environment—anything visible becomes a candidate. The uncomfortable possibility is that many of the problems we attribute to our equipment originate elsewhere. The golfer who changes drivers may improve because confidence has returned, not because the driver is better. The same renewal explains every switch that works: novelty sharpens attention for a while, and lower expectations free the hand. Sometimes the tool matters. Often the change matters more than the tool. What the golfer calls a better driver may be renewed belief.

This can be discouraging or liberating. Discouraging, because no permanent answer waits in the garage: no club is perfect enough to eliminate uncertainty, no purchase finally solves the deeper challenges of the game. Liberating, because the search can end. The problem was never that Driver A was flawed and Driver B perfect. The problem was expecting either one to carry responsibilities that belong to the golfer.

Many experienced players arrive, in time, at a quieter relationship with their equipment. They still appreciate good tools and may even enjoy collecting them, but they stop expecting transformation from them. A club becomes what it always was: a tool, a useful and perhaps beloved one, but a tool.

The point is plainest among players of hickory golf, whose clubs are often decades older than they are. The equipment is fixed, limited and imperfect, and there is little room for fantasies about revolutionary technology. Attention shifts instead toward adaptation. The question becomes not "How can this club save me?" but "What shot does this club invite me to play?" That may be a useful question well outside golf.

The backup club will always be tempting. There will always be another system, another tool, another place, another plan, and sometimes the new thing is genuinely better. Often it is merely free of the burden of recent disappointment, and wisdom consists of telling the difference. When faith in A collapses, moving to B can help; a change of perspective is sometimes exactly what is needed. The mistake is believing that B possesses some magic A lacked. Soon enough B will acquire its own history of failures, and one day, looking back toward A, we will once again remember only the good shots.

The clubs have not changed.

We have.
