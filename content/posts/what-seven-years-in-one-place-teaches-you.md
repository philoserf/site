You learned the year not by calendars but by the willow. When its branches flushed yellow-green at the tips, you could set seeds in the cold frames. When its leaves darkened and thickened and the catkins dropped, you stopped watering the rosemary and let it harden for what was coming. The willow was a clock with one hand, and it had never been wrong.

There were other clocks. The angle of light on the kitchen wall at midmorning told you whether you'd crossed the equinox yet or only imagined it. The pitch of the creek behind the shed—high and conversational in March, sulky and low by August, silent in the deepest weeks of January when ice sealed it shut like an envelope—told you what the rain had done and would do. You knew the wind by which door rattled. Southeast meant the one to the mudroom. Northwest, the one nobody used, the one that faced the hill.

None of this was mystical. It was just what happened when you stopped moving.

Seven years in one place and the place begins to live inside you, or you begin to live inside it, and the distinction stops mattering. The soil under the northeast corner of the garden was clay two inches down, would not drain, and every spring you cursed it and every spring you planted the comfrey there anyway because comfrey didn't care. It thrived on spite and clay. You understood each other.

You knew where the frost settled first: the low hollow between the shed and the fence, where cold air pooled like water. You knew it the way you knew where your knee ached before a storm. The knowledge was in the body. Stepping outside at dusk in October, you could feel the night coming not as temperature but as weight, a pressure that started at the back of the skull and moved downward through the chest. You'd bring in the last tomatoes without checking the forecast. The forecast was your spine.

The old names for this were animism, or superstition, or hedge-witchery. The accurate name is just attention. Seven years of unbroken attention to one set of stimuli, and the stimuli become grammar. The cardinal that sang from the same post every May wasn't a symbol. It was a word in a sentence you'd spent years learning to parse. And the sentence said: the world is not indifferent. The world is specific. The world is happening _here_, to _this_ clay, under _this_ light, and it has been doing so for longer than you and will continue after, and your only task is to not look away.

The transfer came in November.

Of course it did. November is when the world is already practicing its vanishing act, when the color drains from the hills and the trees go skeletal and everything conspires to say: _this was always temporary_. You packed in two weeks. The company gave you three, but two was enough because what you couldn't box wasn't coming with you. You couldn't box the willow. You couldn't box the particular way the 4:30 light hit the western window and turned the dust motes into a slow, gold catastrophe. You couldn't box the cardinal, or the clay, or the fact that you knew exactly how many steps it was from the back door to the creek in the dark, barefoot, without thinking, the way you knew how to breathe.

You drove east for nine hours and arrived at a place that was flat.

The new place had no willow. It had Bradford pears, which bloomed early and smelled like rot and broke in every ice storm because they'd been engineered for beauty and not for endurance. You hated them immediately and specifically. They were the wrong trees in the wrong place, and you recognized in your hatred the absurd projection of a grief you didn't have vocabulary for. The word _homesick_ didn't work. It was too small, too domestic. What you had was something closer to _faith-sick_—the disorientation of someone whose liturgy has been revoked.

Because that's what it was. The daily practice of attention to a specific place is a liturgy. The garden was a chapel. The creek was a hymn. The cardinal was a call to prayer you never asked for and couldn't refuse. And liturgy, once internalized, doesn't care that you've moved. The body keeps performing it. You still woke at the hour when light would have hit the old kitchen wall. You still listened for the creek when it rained, and heard instead the flat, characterless rush of water in a gutter that meant nothing, that told you nothing about the season or the soil or the history of anything.

The new yard was bermuda grass over compacted fill dirt. You pressed your thumb into it and it resisted like a grudge. There was nothing underneath. No clay, no earthworms threading through the dark like slow rumors, no mycelium tying the roots together in an underground parliament. Just fill. Just the dead substrate of construction. You could plant in it the way you could write a poem in a language you didn't speak: technically, with effort, and with the knowledge that the thing produced would lack what makes living things alive.

The resistance lasted months. You kept the old clock running in the new place, kept watching for signals that weren't there, kept parsing a grammar that this landscape didn't speak. The light here was wrong—not wrong, different, but the body doesn't distinguish between different and wrong. It only knows _this is not the pattern_. You found yourself correcting the new place in your mind, as if it were a student making errors. _The sun shouldn't set there. The wind shouldn't come from that direction. February shouldn't feel like this._ The arrogance was staggering. You were a guest insisting the host rearrange the furniture.

You recognized it and couldn't stop.

This is what they don't tell you about a deep attachment to place: it makes you a fundamentalist. You develop a theology of _here_, a conviction that _this_ particular arrangement of light and soil and moving water is not merely where you happen to be but how the world is _supposed_ to work. Every other arrangement is heresy. You become a zealot for one creek, one clay, one willow. And when the world, which owes you nothing, puts you somewhere else, you have the two options available to every fundamentalist: double down, or convert.

Conversion came not as a decision but as an erosion.

April. Something bloomed in the neighbor's yard that you couldn't identify. You walked over and stared at it. White flowers, small, in clusters, with a fragrance like clove and honey. You didn't know the name. You didn't know when it bloomed or how long the bloom lasted or what it meant about the weeks to come. You knew nothing about it. And something in that blankness—that absolute ignorance—cracked the theology open. Because you remembered this feeling. This was how the old place started. Not with knowledge but with not-knowing. Not with the liturgy but with the silence before the liturgy, the dumb staring at a world that hadn't yet become legible.

You'd forgotten that the beginning of all devotion is bewilderment.

You asked the neighbor about the tree. She said it was a _Viburnum_, and it bloomed every year around Easter, and the bees went out of their minds for it, and if you cut the branches and brought them inside they'd last a week in water. This was information. It was not yet knowledge. Knowledge would take years. You'd need to watch it fail to bloom after a late frost, watch it recover, watch the bees come or not come depending on whether the cold snapped the nectar flow. You'd need to learn its relationship to the soil, to the angle of light on this lot at this latitude, to the specific microclimate produced by the neighbor's house to the west and the chain-link fence to the south. You'd need to learn it the way you'd learned the willow, which is to say: slowly, bodily, with no shortcuts.

The old priests called this _kenosis_—an emptying out. You empty yourself of the god you knew so the god you don't know yet has somewhere to arrive.

It took two years.

Two years before the new place began to cohere into something like a language. Two years before you could read the sky here, before you understood what the wind meant when it came from the south instead of the north, before you knew the soil's grudges and generosities, before you'd catalogued the birds and assigned them to their posts. Two years before the light on the wall at midmorning meant anything other than light on a wall.

But it happened. Not as a replacement—the old place was still there, still vivid, still the first grammar, the mother tongue. The new place was a second language, learned in adulthood, never quite as fluent, always carrying the faint accent of the first. But spoken. Lived in. Adequate to prayer.

You planted comfrey in the northeast corner of the new garden. The soil was wrong for it—too sandy, too alkaline, too forgiving. Comfrey wants resistance. Comfrey wants to fight. But you planted it anyway, because some liturgies survive translation, and because the act of kneeling in dirt and pressing a root into the ground is the same act in any language. It is the body saying to the earth: _I am here. I am not leaving. Teach me what you are._

The comfrey grew. It always does. It doesn't care where it is. It only cares that someone put it in the ground and meant it.

There is no moral. Or the moral is this: the pagan instinct—the one that says _this hill is sacred, this spring is holy, this tree is the axis of the world_—is both the deepest truth and the deepest trap. It is true that a place can become sacred through attention. It is a trap to believe that only one place can. The sacred is not a property of the landscape. It is a property of the relationship between the landscape and the one who refuses to look away.

Travel breaks the gaze. Displacement severs the thread. And you can spend years trying to re-tie it to the old post, feeding the knot with memory and grief, keeping the faith by keeping the wound open.

Or you can turn around. Look at the ground you're standing on. Kneel, and learn its name.
