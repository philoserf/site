Claims of an "AI bubble" usually misread what the money is doing. The bulk of current spending isn't a bet on distant possibilities. It's payment for infrastructure that generates revenue and efficiency **right now**.

Firms are buying compute, models, and tooling they use immediately. Cloud platforms, chip makers, and software vendors capture that spend as revenue today. This is not the psychology of a bubble; it's the economics of capacity building.

What confuses observers is pricing, not fundamentals. We still lack a stable sense of what AI output should cost—marginal inference prices fluctuate, model capabilities expand unevenly, and hardware cycles compress. Markets guess. Volatility follows. None of that implies speculative waste; it reflects the difficulty of pricing a general-purpose technology during its formative stage.

The bubble analogy persists for three simpler reasons.

- It's familiar: commentators reach for dot-com metaphors even though the situation differs—AI systems are already deployed at scale and producing measurable output.
- Costs appear before the gains: you see GPU clusters long before efficiency shows up in quarterly reports.
- Narrative gravity: "bubble" makes for a cleaner story than "transitional infrastructure investment with uneven productivity capture."

A better line of inquiry looks at where the investment **fails** or **stalls**.

- Which categories of spending are wasted—excess capacity, misaligned tooling, unused models?
- Where does adoption slow—regulation, integration debt, workflow redesign, trust barriers?
- What lets productivity become visible—better measurement, clearer unit economics, or AI-native processes?

These questions cut closer to reality than the bubble/no-bubble debate. The transition is uneven, not irrational. The money is building capacity; the challenge is turning that capacity into durable productivity.
