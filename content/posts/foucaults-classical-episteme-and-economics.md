title: Foucault's Classical Episteme and Economics
---
In The Order of Things Michel Foucault argued that the Classical episteme rendered the subject invisible: knowledge was organized around representation and order, not around human consciousness as the source of meaning.

In the period Foucault calls Classical, roughly 1650 to 1800, knowledge took the form of tables. Things were classified and ordered by their visible characteristics; language was transparent nomenclature, words simply naming what existed. The human being who observed and named was absent from the framework, present everywhere as the one who classified and nowhere as a thing classified. Man existed not as a problem to be studied but as the unexamined condition of study itself—the eye that cannot see itself seeing.

On this account, questions about perception and bias could not even be formulated. A framework that excludes the subject naturalizes its own stance: knowledge appears objective and universal while every act of understanding remains situated.

The same blindness recurs wherever a system renders us invisible to ourselves, and economics ran the whole sequence again without knowing it. The rational-actor model is a Classical table: agents classified by preferences and constraints, choices ordered by utility, the choosing mind nowhere in the framework. For a century the model’s builders stood outside it exactly as the Classical taxonomist stood outside his table. Then behavioral economics repeated the nineteenth-century move Foucault describes—what he called the invention of man—by installing the subject as an object of study. Kahneman and Tversky put the observer’s own cognition on the table; Thaler built a subfield on the gap between the modeled agent and the actual one. The defect is inherited, not general: it descends from the Classical move, and each new framework that dissolves the subject reopens it. Economics closed its blind spot only by rediscovering, without reading him, the sequence Foucault had already mapped.
