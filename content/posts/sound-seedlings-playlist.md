---
title: Sound Seedlings Playlist
description: A curated 77-min instrumental playlist structured as a two-crest arc from ambient to downtempo, funk, cinematic, and back.
date: 2026-06-20
---

Sound Seedlings is built to be heard as a single descent and return rather than a collection—a journey that begins below the waterline, surfaces twice, and submerges again on its own terms. The runtime is a shade over 77 minutes across 15 instrumental tracks, and the shape is deliberate enough that shuffling it would dismantle most of what makes it work.

It opens with Dustin O'Halloran's "An Ending, A Beginning," and the title is doing structural duty: at two minutes it is the shortest thing here, a fragment of solo piano and strings that functions as a threshold, not a track. Nothing is asked of the listener yet. It establishes the register—instrumental, unhurried, emotionally legible—and then hands off to Nils Frahm's "Says," which is the first real act of the set. Across its eight minutes "Says" performs the move the whole playlist will rely on in miniature: it begins in near-stillness, a single arpeggiated figure, and accretes rhythm and density until what started as ambient piano is unmistakably pulsing. Its placement is the load-bearing decision of the entire order—the bridge from silence into motion, earning a transition that a colder, beat-first opener could not.

From there the body settles. Wintergatan's "Marble Machine" and Kodomo's "Concept 1" ease the listener onto a downtempo plateau without yet committing to a groove; "Marble Machine" carries a mechanical, music-box brightness that keeps the texture from going heavy too early. Then the playlist finds its center of gravity in a run of four mid-tempo cuts: Kruder & Dorfmeister's "Definition," Dølle Jølle's "Balearic Incarnation," Fila Brazillia's "Harmonicas are Shite," and Nightmares On Wax's "Morse." This stretch, roughly 24 minutes, is the chill-out heart of the set, and it is intentionally flat in the best sense—Vienna and UK downtempo lineage, dub-inflected, hazy, designed to be lived inside rather than tracked moment to moment. It is also the stretch most vulnerable to a listener who wants incident: the four tracks share enough tempo and atmosphere that they blur, and the blur is the point only if you accept that the playlist's job here is environmental. If the set has a weakness, this is it, and the weakness is inseparable from the function.

The first crest breaks that haze on purpose. The Herbaliser's "The Sensual Woman" is the funkiest, most breakbeat-forward cut in the set, and it lifts straight into St Germain's "Sure Thing," the lone jazz-house moment, organ-warm and propulsive. Putting them back to back is the right move: both are energy spikes, and scattering them would have produced two jolts instead of one peak. Together they read as a single deliberate surge—the set briefly remembering it can dance—before Nightmares On Wax returns with "You Wish" to bring the temperature back down. That reappearance is the only repeated artist, and the two tracks are spaced and differentiated enough that it registers as a recurring pulse rather than padding.

The second crest is cinematic rather than funky. Nigel Stanford's "Automatica" re-energizes after the descent and flows directly into "The Edge," the longest track at nearly nine minutes. Sequencing these two consecutively is what lets "The Edge" function as the emotional climax: it has room to build because "Automatica" has already set the cinematic frame. "Automatica" belongs here in a way it could not have as an opener—too percussive for a cold start in a set this mellow, but as a re-ignition feeding a long build, exactly right.

The landing is unhurried. IXI's "Siberia" pulls the cinematic build back into ambient suspension, and Bonobo's "Elysian"—three minutes, soft, warm—sets it down. After two crests and a long descent, the set does not resolve on a button; it dissolves, which is the correct ending for music that began below the waterline.

The architecture, then, is a two-crest curve framed by hush at both ends: a threshold, a build, a long plateau, a funk-and-house peak, a cinematic peak, and an ambient release. The discipline lies in the separation of extremes: the two longest tracks ("Says" and "The Edge") sit as far apart as possible so neither stalls the flow, and the two energy spikes are clustered, not dispersed, so contrast registers as structure rather than interruption. One honest caveat: all of this only holds in order. Shuffled, the playlist collapses into an undifferentiated downtempo wash—pleasant, but arbitrary—and the entire case for the sequence rests on hearing it start to finish.
