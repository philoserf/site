# A theory of the philoserf.com site

## What the system actually is

This repository is **the rendering half of a two-repo writing pipeline**, not a website project in the conventional sense. The author writes in an Obsidian vault of ~2,400 notes living in a sibling directory (`../notes`). A plugin in another sibling (`../obsidian-publisher`) detects notes where `status: publish` is set, transforms them into Hugo-flavored markdown, and writes them into this repo's `content/posts/`. This repo's job is to receive that traffic, render it without complaint, and serve it to GitHub Pages. The 894-commit history is dominated by `Publish 109 notes from Obsidian` style commits; those are not commits this repo authored, they are _deliveries it accepted_.

The domain this code models is therefore not "blog" but **"public projection of a personal knowledge graph."** The entities are: an author (singular, named in `hugo.yaml`), a body of essays (whatever the publisher has currently decided is `status: publish`), and four hand-curated structural pages — `_index.md`, `about.md`, `now.md`, `uses.md` — that describe the author rather than convey their thinking. Everything in this repo is either the author's identity (config, structural pages, layout), a contract with the publisher (shortcodes, fallback partials), or scaffolding to ship the output (build, deploy). There is no editorial layer, no draft workflow internal to the site, no analytics, no comments. The site is a deliberately thin projection.

## Organizing ideas

**Identity is data; every public artifact is a projection of it.** `hugo.yaml`'s `params` block (author, email, since-year, motto, full vCard contact details, social URLs) is the source of truth. From it the home page emits _five_ artifacts under `outputs.home`: `html`, `rss`, `llms`, `manifest`, `vcard`. Each non-HTML artifact has a paired template at `layouts/index.<name>.<ext>` and a registered output format in `hugo.yaml`. `robots.txt` is similarly templated (one file at `layouts/robots.txt`). The key consequence: changing the author's name updates the home page, the RSS feed, the LLM index, the PWA manifest, the vCard, the JSON-LD on every post, the OpenGraph `article:author` on posts, and the copyright footer — in one edit. That fan-out is the design's central economy, and it is why `static/` contains no `vcard.vcf`, no `site.webmanifest`, and no `llms.txt`: any such file would silently override the templated projection.

**The publisher's contract is paid in graceful degradation, not validation.** Hugo does no front-matter validation, on purpose. `layouts/partials/description.html` reads `.Description → .Summary → .Site.Params.description`, so a missing description never breaks rendering. `layouts/_default/single.html` and `baseof.html` guard every date/time with `.Date.IsZero` and `.Lastmod.IsZero`. The JSON-LD builder uses `merge` to conditionally add `datePublished` and `dateModified`. None of these are defensive habits; they are the **interface specification**. The publisher is allowed to change its mind about what fields it emits, and the rendering side must absorb the change. The "schema" lives in `../obsidian-publisher/src/schema.ts`, not here.

**The two archetypes are not duplication, they are an ontological commitment.** `archetypes/default.md` scaffolds `lastmod` only; `archetypes/posts.md` scaffolds `date` only. The asymmetry encodes that structural pages have a _most-recently-edited_ moment but no birthday, while essays have a birthday and let `enableGitInfo: true` derive their last-modified from git history. `single.html` keys off `.Date.IsZero` to decide whether to render `<time datetime=…>`, so an essay scaffolded with the wrong archetype would silently render dateless. A maintainer who collapses these into one template will damage the model without breaking the build.

**Two disabling decisions are part of the design, not omissions.** `taxonomies: {}` is empty with an inline comment — there are no tags and no categories. The publisher does emit a `tags` field on posts; the site reads it and discards it. JSON-LD models posts as `BlogPosting` with a single `author` and `publisher` both pointing at the same `Person`. The model is _one author, no categorization_. A request for tags, co-authors, or a taxonomy page is not an additive request — it requires renegotiating the model. The other disabling decision is the absence of any client-side JavaScript by default. Mermaid and KaTeX load conditionally (see below), and nothing else loads at all.

**Conditional asset loading is the second economy.** `layouts/_default/baseof.html` loads KaTeX only when `.Params.math` or `.Site.Params.math` is true (via `partials/math.html`), and loads `mermaid.js` only when `.HasShortcode "mermaid"`. Math is opted into per-page (currently three posts: dating-age-range-formula, goal-persistence-model, ai-agents-and-opportunity-cost). Mermaid is opted in by the act of using the shortcode. This pattern is used exactly twice and is the established way to add a heavyweight dependency; an unconditional `<script>` in `baseof.html` would violate it.

**Strictness is the integration test.** There is no test suite, no link checker, no snapshot test. There is `hugo --panicOnWarning`. It runs in `task check` and in the deploy workflow. Commits `10b6caa` and `b20b17b` hardened this in CI and locally within days of each other in late April. This flag is the closest thing this repo has to a CI guard, and it specifically protects the publisher seam: a shortcode the publisher emits but the site does not define is a Hugo warning, and `panicOnWarning` turns it into a deploy failure rather than a silently rendered literal `{{< callout >}}` in the live page.

**Shortcodes split into two ownership tiers.** `latin-motto.html` is local — owned outright; it renders the shared `motto.html` partial, which sources the lines from `params.motto`. `callout.html` and `mermaid.html` are _contract templates_: they exist because the publisher emits these tags. Their names (`callout`, `mermaid`) match the publisher's `calloutShortcodeName` and `mermaidShortcodeName` defaults in `../obsidian-publisher/src/types.ts`. The publisher exposes these as settings, so the contract is _configurable on both sides_ — change one without the other and rendered output diverges from authored intent. CLAUDE.md's instruction to "keep in sync with `../obsidian-publisher/hugo-shortcodes/`" is naming exactly this contract.

## Seams

**Publisher → `content/posts/` is the most important seam and is invisible from inside this repo.** It is a one-way overwrite. The only traces visible here are the commit messages (`Publish 95 notes from Obsidian`, etc.) and the absence of editorial activity on individual posts in the log. A maintainer who edits `content/posts/foo.md` will lose that edit on the next publish. The convention enforced by `task new:post` — that local scaffolding goes to `content/posts/` — is fundamentally in tension with the publisher's behavior, but the tension never fires because the author does not actually use `task new:post`; the publisher creates posts.

**Home page → custom output formats is principled and reusable.** The recipe has four moving parts: register a media type in `hugo.yaml`'s `mediaTypes`, register an output format under `outputFormats` with `notAlternative: true` (so it does not appear as `<link rel="alternate">` in the `<head>`, which would clutter the HTML head with non-discoverable bot-facing files), list the format in `outputs.home`, and provide a template at `layouts/index.<format>.<extension>`. This is how `llms.txt`, `site.webmanifest`, and `vcard.vcf` all get produced. New home-page projections follow this same shape exactly.

**Deploy is shallow but worth knowing precisely.** GitHub Pages, artifact-based (`actions/upload-pages-artifact` + `actions/deploy-pages`), no `gh-pages` branch. Triggered by push to `main`, by manual dispatch, and by a daily cron at `25 8 * * *` (i.e., 08:25 UTC). The cron is unexplained in its commit but the most likely reason is that `vcard.vcf` carries `REV:{{ now.Format ... }}`, so a daily build keeps that timestamp fresh; the rendered output formats are otherwise pure functions of inputs that change only on publish.

**Two CI workflows, one strictness level.** `.github/workflows/deploy.yml` and `.github/workflows/build.yml` both run `hugo --gc --minify --panicOnWarning` and both install Hugo via `peaceiris/actions-hugo@v3` — _intentionally unpinned_, per the inline comment "tracks latest like Homebrew." Pre-merge feedback (build.yml, on PRs) exercises the same build the deploy will run; the differences are triggers only, plus deploy's lychee link check and Pages upload.

**Repo-level settings are governed by the meta-repo.** `.github/settings.yml` extends `philoserf/.github` via probot/settings, declaring the repo name, description, homepage, and topics. Editing repository settings via the GitHub UI will be overwritten. The actual settings — including branch protection if any — live one repo over.

**Dependency pinning policy is intentionally inconsistent.** `static/mermaid.js` pins `mermaid@10.9.1` with a comment explaining how to bump. The KaTeX CDN URL pins `katex@0.16.11` with SRI hashes. Both workflows' Hugo action is unpinned and tracks latest. Brewfile (used by `task setup`) installs latest from Homebrew. The pattern: **client-side assets are pinned for reproducibility; build tooling tracks upstream.** This is a deliberate choice — local builds, CI builds, and the user's browser all tolerate "latest" Hugo and Task, but a visitor's browser cannot tolerate a silently moving Mermaid runtime.

## What changes easily

Updating site identity is one edit in `hugo.yaml`'s `params` block and propagates everywhere. Adding a structural page is a markdown file under `content/`, plus an entry in `hugo.yaml`'s `menus.main`, plus optionally an `llmsSection: writing` param to control how it appears in `llms.txt`. Adding a new home-page output format follows the four-step recipe above. Tweaking visual style is `static/style.css` (uses CSS custom properties for theming, supports `prefers-color-scheme: dark` via redefined tokens). Opting a page into KaTeX is one front-matter line. Adding a shortcode the publisher will emit is `layouts/shortcodes/<name>.html` plus a matching change on the publisher side.

## What does not change easily

Anything that introduces _per-post mutable state in this repo_ will fight the publisher's overwrite: view counts, edit timestamps maintained locally, comment threads, build-time augmentation of post content. The publisher does not read this repo; it writes to it.

Anything that introduces a _second writer_ into `content/posts/` requires deciding what happens when the two collide. Today there is exactly one writer per path: the publisher for `posts/*.md`, the human for the four structural pages.

Anything that _removes the publisher's degradation guarantees_ — a strict required-field check on front matter, a template that errors instead of falling back on missing `.Date` — will turn the publisher's next release into a deploy failure. The publisher does not version-negotiate with this repo.

_Adding taxonomies_ requires more than uncommenting `taxonomies: {}`. The publisher emits `tags` already, but the site has chosen one-author, no-categorization as a model decision. Adding tag pages means designing a list layout for taxonomy terms, deciding where the menu entry goes, deciding whether tags appear on individual posts (currently they do not), and renegotiating the JSON-LD model.

A maintainer with the theory looks first at `hugo.yaml` for identity, `layouts/_default/` for the three page kinds (`baseof` / `single` / `list`), `layouts/index.*` for the home-page-as-multi-format-projector, and `archetypes/` to understand the date semantics. A maintainer without the theory edits a post directly under `content/posts/`, loses it on the next publish, and concludes Hugo is buggy.

## Uncertainties and tensions

The daily 08:25 UTC cron has no rationale committed. The vCard `REV:` timestamp is the most plausible reason; `enableGitInfo: true` populating `Lastmod` from git is a less compelling reason because git history does not advance on quiet days. I am inferring; the commit message did not say.

The project CLAUDE.md asserts the cron is "15:25 UTC" but the workflow file is `25 8 * * *` (08:25 UTC). One of them is stale. The workflow is authoritative; CLAUDE.md is documentation drift, probably from a daylight-saving conversion done in someone's head.

The publisher's neighbor doc `../notes/Publishing Pipeline.md` still warns that the publisher emits `{{< notice >}}` and the site does not define it. That warning is **now obsolete**: the publisher was updated to default `calloutShortcodeName: "callout"` and `mermaidShortcodeName: "mermaid"` (see `../obsidian-publisher/src/types.ts`), and the site grew matching shortcodes in commit `07f92b7`. The contract is currently coherent; the cross-repo theory document is out of date.

`task build` runs `hugo --minify` while the deploy workflow runs `hugo --gc --minify --panicOnWarning`. Local builds do not enforce strictness; only `task check` does. A maintainer who relies on `task build` to validate their work will miss exactly the class of bug the strict flag exists to catch.

The vCard at `layouts/index.vcard.vcf` includes home street address, mobile phone number, and date of birth. The author has confirmed twice that this is intentional — a deliberately full-fidelity contact card. A code-audit tool will keep flagging it; the answer is keep dismissing.

The `notAlternative: true` flag on all three custom output formats has no documented rationale, but the behavior is unambiguous: it keeps these formats out of `<link rel="alternate">` entries in the HTML head. The intent reads as "these are bot- and external-discovery surfaces, not user-discoverable representations of the page." I am inferring.

`theory.md` and `walkthrough.md` were both added in the last three days. The previous `THEORY.md` was deleted as stale and the new one written from scratch. That cycle suggests both documents are treated as living artifacts that go out of date and get rewritten — they are projections of current understanding, not durable contracts. Treat this theory the same way. The parts most likely to remain true are the publisher relationship, the archetype asymmetry, the conditional-asset pattern, and the templated-identity fan-out. The parts most likely to drift are the specific output formats (more may be added), the cron schedule (it has moved at least once), and the exact shortcode names (they are settings on both sides).
